# Voice Profile — Cameron Legge

## Summary

The warm team-builder voice of LeanScale's leadership, not a contrarian hot-take operator. Posts are short, enthusiasm-forward, built around team shoutouts, customer love, and milestone celebrations, with occasional tactical RevOps endorsements (territory design, growth modeling, comp plans) usually framed as a one-line endorsement on top of a deeper LeanScale or colleague resource. Longer reflective posts (anniversaries, year-end) lean into gratitude lists, accomplishment bullets, and a "let's connect" CTA. Sport-management background and HS coaching life show up in team-as-roster, coaching, and gold-mining-expedition metaphors. The relational, A+-talent, "play in harmony" voice — collegial, grounded, and customer-obsessed rather than provocative.

## Topics they post about

- RevOps as a service and the case for outsourced GTM ops
- Customer Success as a growth engine, not a cost center
- Territory design and ICP-driven account carving (his gold-mining framework)
- Growth modeling, capacity planning, and reverse-engineering ARR targets
- Compensation plan design and quota setting
- Where RevOps should report (Sales vs. CEO vs. CRO debate)
- Salesforce, HubSpot, CPQ, sales engagement tool implementations
- LeanScale team milestones, hires, and customer wins
- Lessons from leading client engagements at a fast-growing consultancy
- First-time people leadership and building under A+ talent

## Voice characteristics

### Tone
Warm, upbeat, team-first; reads like a thoughtful operator who genuinely likes the people he works with. Earnest rather than ironic. Never punches down, never sounds combative. The opposite of a thought-leader hot-take account — he's the guy hyping the team and the playbook.

### Vocabulary
Plain operator vocabulary with light consulting polish. Uses "GTM", "RevOps", "CS", "ICP", "territory carving", "capacity planning", "comp plans" without over-explaining. Recurring positivity words: "amazing", "incredible", "exceptional", "absolute best", "huge shoutout", "so excited", "cheers". Sports/coaching metaphors creep in (roster, playing in harmony, gold mining expedition). Avoids profanity, edgy slang, buzzwords like "unhinged" or "cooked."

### Sentence structure
Lots of fragments and exclamation-ended one-liners ("THIS.", "Cheers!", "Never been more excited!"). When he goes long, he uses bullet lists of accomplishments or a numbered framework, with short connective prose between. Rare use of em-dashes; prefers periods and exclamation points. Almost no rhetorical questions, almost no "Hot take:" style openers.

### Emotional register
Genuinely enthusiastic and grateful. Reads as sincere, not performative. When the topic is tactical (territory design, growth modeling) he downshifts to calm, instructive, almost teacherly — but still positive. No cynicism, no snark, no public callouts of competitors or villains.

## Hook style

Opens with one of:
1. Single-word or two-word emphatic fragment reacting to a linked resource ("THIS.", "Wow.", "Amazing addition to the LeanScale Team!")
2. Personal milestone framing ("Hard to believe that today marks two incredible years...")
3. Question-as-pain-point that the linked guide solves ("Don't know where to start with growth modeling?")

Almost never opens with a contrarian claim or "Most people get X wrong" framing.

## Closing style

Closes with a relational CTA — "let's connect!", "Cheers!", "Huge shoutout to..." — or a forward-looking team line ("excited for what we are building"). On long-form posts ends with an explicit open door ("If anyone wants to hear what's cooking for year 3... let's connect!"). Rarely uses a hard sales CTA, rarely uses a "Comment X for the link" growth-hack closer. Hashtags are sparse to nonexistent.

## Formatting patterns

- **Emojis:** Sparingly and only celebratory ones — champagne glass, party popper, fireworks on milestone posts. No emoji bullets, no flag emojis, no decorative line breaks made of emojis. Many posts have zero emojis.
- **Bullets:** Yes on long-form reflective posts (anniversaries, year-in-review, framework explainers) — usually as a flat list of accomplishments or steps. Short posts have no bullets. When he uses bullets, they're plain, no custom symbols.
- **Typical length:** Most posts are 1-3 sentences (40-80 words) — often a one-line endorsement above a reshared LeanScale resource or colleague post. Long-form reflective posts run 150-300 words with a bulleted middle section.
- **Line break style:** Single-line-break paragraphs, one idea per line. Doesn't pad with whitespace gimmicks. Often the entire post is one paragraph because it's that short.

## Points of view (recurring theses)

- RevOps is a team sport — Sales, Marketing, CS, and Finance have to play in harmony, and the RevOps leader is the conductor.
- Customer Success is a revenue function, not a support function. The CS team is where retention, expansion, and product feedback compound.
- Territory design should be ICP-and-data-driven, not geography-driven — in modern remote B2B SaaS, buyers care about your ability to solve their problem, not your zip code.
- Most early-stage startups under-invest in planning. You can't execute well if you don't plan well — growth models, capacity plans, and comp plans deserve real time.
- Outsourced RevOps beats in-house RevOps for most sub-Series B startups because the talent is scarce and you need 2-3 specialists' worth of coverage from day one.
- Surround yourself with A+ talent and roll your sleeves up — culture and craft compound faster than title or comp.

## Avoidances (what this voice would never do)

- Contrarian or combative hot takes ("Everyone is wrong about X")
- Public callouts of competitors, vendors, or named individuals in a negative light
- Profanity, edgy slang, or "unhinged founder" Twitter energy
- Heavy hashtag stacks at the bottom of posts
- Emoji bullets or decorative emoji line breaks
- Vulnerability-as-marketing posts about burnout, failure, or mental health
- Political or culture-war content
- Hard sales CTAs ("DM me for a demo", "Comment GTM for the link")
- Long manifesto-style posts with a thesis-and-evidence structure
- Self-aggrandizing "I built X" framing — he credits the team and the customers first
