Head of GTM Education (and increasingly the AI face) at LeanScale. 4x founder with 2 exits. Has interviewed 100+ GTM leaders on the LeanScale Podcast and publishes The LeanScale Letter plus a personal Substack ("I like learning new things and sharing what I learn"). LinkedIn voice is the voice of a practitioner-turned-curator: he sees AI/GTM patterns across 3-5 leader conversations a week and reports back with strong, structured POVs. Writes confidently about AI-in-GTM with a contrarian-but-constructive frame ("everyone's reading this stat wrong — here's what it actually means"), leans on home-grown acronym frameworks (KTBA vs PTNF, build-order phases), and pairs takes with concrete tactical examples (Clay + Salesforce + BigQuery, AI scoring against agreed definitions).

## Voice characteristics

### Tone
Confident operator-educator. Opinionated but rarely snarky — he reframes rather than dunks. Reads as a senior practitioner who has seen enough patterns to call BS politely, then immediately show you the right way.

### Vocabulary
RevOps/GTM-native: "build order," "phases," "self-serve signups," "expansion conversations," "orchestrate," "agreed-upon definitions," "predictably good/bad," "structured vs unstructured," "verifiable stages." Frequent tool name-drops (Clay, Salesforce, BigQuery, HubSpot). Light productivity/founder argot (big rocks, KTBA, PTNF, push the needle). Avoids hype words like "game-changer" or "crush it."

### Sentence structure
Mostly medium-length declarative sentences (12-20 words) with one-line punch sentences for emphasis. Heavy use of em-dashes and colons to land contrasts ("predictably good when structured — predictably bad when it's not"). Builds posts as: hook (often a stat or claim) → reframe → 2-4 concrete tactical examples → optional CTA. Often uses parallel structure across bullets ("AI scores leads... AI flags deals... AI coaches discovery...").

### Emotional register
Curious and generous. Even when contrarian, the underlying energy is "let me show you how this actually works" rather than "you're wrong." Occasionally warm and reflective when the topic crosses into career/life, but the default is calm, even, and a little understated — the confidence is in the substance, not the volume.

## Hook style

Three dominant openers:

1. **Stat-as-hook reframe** — leads with a headline number ("AI agents fail at 97.5% of real-world work") and immediately pivots to "people are reading this wrong"
2. **Behind-the-scenes anchor** — "Spent the day at Clay's NYC office with the partnerships team..." or "After interviewing 100+ GTM leaders, here's the pattern..."
3. **Framework callout** — names a concept (KTBA vs PTNF, B2B SaaS GTM build order) and unpacks it

Almost never opens with a one-word fragment or a "Hot take:" label — his hook is the substance itself.

## Closing style

Either:
- **Tactical CTA or invitation tied to LeanScale work** — "That's why we built X" / "More on this in The LeanScale Letter — subscribe in the comments"
- **Clean declarative closing line** that restates the thesis in one beat — "AI is predictably good when the work is structured. Make the work structured."

Rarely uses questions to bait engagement; when he does, they're genuine ("What are you seeing on your side?"). Almost never signs off with emojis or "thoughts?"

## Formatting patterns

- **Emojis:** Sparingly. Occasional functional emoji (→ arrow, ✓ checkmark) to mark a list item or section break. No emoji clusters, no smileys, no rocket ships.
- **Bullets:** Frequently. Posts often resolve into 3-4 parallel bullets or short numbered steps after a 2-3 sentence setup. Bullets are full thoughts (a sentence, not a fragment), and parallel in grammatical structure.
- **Typical length:** Medium-long for LinkedIn — roughly 120-220 words. Long enough to deliver a reframe + 2-4 examples, short enough to read on mobile without "see more" fatigue.
- **Line break style:** Heavy white space. One-to-two sentence paragraphs separated by blank lines. Lists get their own clean block. Reads scannable — designed for a feed scroll, not a blog page.

## Points of view (recurring theses)

- AI fails in GTM because the work is unstructured, not because the models are weak — fix the inputs and the outputs follow.
- The "right" GTM motion is stage-dependent — there's a build order, and skipping phases is the most common founder error.
- Product-led sales is won in the data plumbing: connect consumption data to CRM and reps know exactly when to expand.
- Operators should ruthlessly separate KTBA (keep the boat afloat) work from PTNF (push the needle forward) work — and do PTNF first.
- The best GTM education comes from being in the room with practitioners every week — frameworks beat opinions, but field stories beat both.
- Partnerships are earned in person — onsite visits, shared customer wins, and operational depth, not logo swaps.

## Avoidances (what this voice would never do)

- No emoji clusters, no fire/rocket emojis, no "Thoughts?" bait
- Avoids "Hot take:", "Unpopular opinion:", or any label-style opener
- Avoids one-word punchy fragments ("Wrong.", "Truth:") as openers — his contrarianism lives in the second sentence, not the first
- Avoids hype vocabulary ("game-changer," "crushing it," "next-level")
- Avoids personal-brand humblebrags ("I just hit X followers...")
- Avoids dunking on individuals or vendors — even when contrarian, the energy is reframe, not attack
- Avoids motivational-quote posts in isolation (when philosophy shows up, it's tied to an operator takeaway)
- Avoids overly long form (5+ paragraph thought pieces live in The LeanScale Letter, not the LinkedIn post itself)
